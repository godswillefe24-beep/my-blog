Rare and Unusual Programming Languages You Probably Haven't Tried

When most developers think about programming, languages like Python, JavaScript, C++, Java, or Rust usually come to mind. However, the programming world is filled with hundreds of lesser-known languages. Some were created as experiments, others for education, and a few simply to challenge the way we think about writing software.

Exploring unusual programming languages may not make you more employable overnight, but they can change how you approach problem solving and expand your understanding of computer science.

1. Brainf*ck

Brainf*ck is famous for having only eight commands. Despite its tiny syntax, it is Turing complete, meaning it can theoretically perform any computation that a modern programming language can. 

++++++++++
[>+++++++>++++++++++>+++>+<<<<-]
>++.>+.+++++++..+++.>++.
<<+++++++++++++++.
>.+++.------.--------.>+.>.

The program above prints: 

Hello World!

2. Whitespace

Whitespace is one of the strangest languages ever created. Its source code consists entirely of spaces, tabs, and newline characters. Everything else is ignored by the compiler. 

[Space][Space][Tab][Newline]
[Tab][Space][Space][Newline]

While nearly impossible to read, it demonstrates that programming syntax doesn't have to be made from visible characters.

3. LOLCODE

LOLCODE is inspired by early internet meme culture and intentionally resembles the language used in classic "LOLcats" memes. 

HAI 1.2

VISIBLE "HAI WORLD!"

KTHXBYE

Output: 

HAI WORLD!

4. Piet

Piet takes a completely different approach. Programs are not written with text—they're paintings made of colored blocks. The flow of the program depends on transitions between colors.

A Piet program looks more like modern art than source code, making it one of the most visually unique programming languages ever designed.

5. Shakespeare Programming Language (SPL)

Imagine writing code as if it were one of Shakespeare's plays. In SPL, variables are characters, and the entire program is structured like a theatrical script. 

Romeo, a young man with remarkable patience.
Juliet, a likewise young woman.

Act I: A beginning.

Scene I:
[Enter Romeo and Juliet]

Romeo:
You are as beautiful as the sum of yourself and a cat.

Juliet:
Speak your mind.

6. Chef

Chef turns programs into cooking recipes. Variables become ingredients, and functions resemble cooking instructions. 

Hello World Souffle.

Ingredients.
101 eggs
111 ml oil

Method.
Put eggs into the mixing bowl.
Liquefy contents of the mixing bowl.
Pour contents into the baking dish.

Serves 1.

Why Do These Languages Exist?

At first glance, these languages may seem like jokes—and some of them certainly are. However, they often have serious educational value. They explore compiler design, language theory, minimal syntax, and alternative ways humans can communicate instructions to computers.

"Sometimes the strangest programming language teaches the most interesting lesson."

Should You Learn One?

If your goal is building websites or mobile apps, probably not. Languages like Python, JavaScript, C#, Go, and Rust will serve you much better professionally.

But if you're curious about how programming languages are designed, spending an afternoon experimenting with Brainf*ck, Piet, or Chef can completely change how you think about software development.

Final Thoughts

The history of programming is full of creativity and experimentation. Rare languages remind us that coding isn't just about solving practical problems—it's also an art form and a playground for new ideas. Even if you never use these languages in a real project, exploring them can make you a more thoughtful programmer and deepen your appreciation for the tools you use every day.
